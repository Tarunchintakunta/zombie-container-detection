## SECTION: Critical Analysis of Anchoring Papers and Identification of Research Gaps

(IEEE-style continuous paragraphs — ready to insert into thesis)

---

Two recent works anchor the present research and motivate the specific contribution of a heuristic-based zombie container detector. The first, by Anemogiannis et al. (2025), addresses anomaly detection across the full Kubernetes resource hierarchy using machine-learning models trained on Prometheus time-series data, while the second, by Li et al. (2025), confronts the energy cost of idle containers through a feedback-control scaling algorithm. Together these papers cover substantial ground in Kubernetes cluster health and resource efficiency, yet neither provides a dedicated, interpretable mechanism for classifying individual containers as zombie instances on the basis of sustained behavioural patterns. The remainder of this section examines each paper in depth, identifies the specific gaps that persist after their contributions, and explains how the heuristic-based approach developed in this thesis fills those gaps in a manner that is lightweight, transparent, and operationally practical.

Anemogiannis et al. (2025) propose a framework in which the topology of a Kubernetes cluster is encoded as a heterogeneous graph stored in Neo4j, with nodes representing Pods, Deployments, ReplicaSets, Namespaces, and physical Nodes, and edges capturing ownership and co-location relationships. Prometheus serves as the telemetry backbone, collecting CPU saturation, memory utilisation, and request-rate metrics at a fixed scrape interval; Grafana provides ancillary visualisation. Anomaly detection proceeds in two phases. In the unsupervised phase, Isolation Forest, DBSCAN, and One-Class SVM are evaluated to define a boundary of normality; in the supervised phase, Decision Trees, Support Vector Machines, and Logistic Regression classify future observations against that boundary. The authors report a best-case silhouette score of 0.794 for Isolation Forest and a best-case F1 score of 0.886 for Decision Trees across N = 100 experimental iterations conducted on the production EO4EU Earth-observation cluster. The evaluation includes fit-time and prediction-time measurements, providing initial evidence that the computational overhead of the supervised classifiers remains manageable in a dynamic cluster.

Several aspects of the Anemogiannis et al. framework are directly relevant to the present work. First, the monitoring stack — Prometheus at a fifteen-second scrape interval feeding CPU and memory metrics — is identical to the stack adopted in this thesis; this congruence permits a fair methodological comparison between machine-learning-based anomaly scores and the heuristic scores produced by the detector developed here. Second, the Isolation Forest results reported in that paper serve as a concrete, published baseline against which the heuristic approach can be benchmarked, which is significant because Isolation Forest is the dominant unsupervised method cited across the anomaly-detection literature for containerised workloads (Cui et al., 2024). Third, the use of graph-based cluster representation and the infrastructure required to maintain it — a Neo4j database, periodic graph-update cycles, Optuna-based hyperparameter tuning — exposes a non-trivial operational cost that is absent from the heuristic system proposed here, which requires only direct Prometheus queries and deterministic rule evaluation.

Despite these strengths, five specific gaps limit the applicability of Anemogiannis et al. (2025) to the zombie container problem. First, the framework is designed to detect active performance degradation, namely CPU saturation spikes and memory leaks that manifest as statistically anomalous deviations from learned normality. It does not address the inverse pattern that characterises zombie containers: sustained, near-zero CPU utilisation accompanied by stable or slowly growing memory allocation and negligible network activity, a behavioural signature that sits well within the statistical norm and would therefore be classified as healthy by Isolation Forest or Decision Tree models trained on cluster-wide distributions. Second, the paper contains no mention of zombie containers, idle containers, orphan resources, or resource waste anywhere in its twenty-seven pages; the concept of a container that is running but performing no useful work is outside its problem formulation. Third, the machine-learning approach is inherently opaque: a Decision Tree of sufficient depth to achieve F1 = 0.886 across heterogeneous workloads is not readily interpretable by a site-reliability engineer who needs to understand why a given container was flagged and whether the flag is actionable. Fourth, the framework introduces a significant infrastructure dependency — the Neo4j graph database — and ongoing model-retraining overhead that may be difficult to justify in resource-constrained environments or in organisations with strict change-control governance. Fifth, the evaluation compares six machine-learning algorithms against one another but never benchmarks any of them against simpler rule-based or threshold-based methods, leaving open the question of whether a lightweight heuristic approach could achieve comparable or superior results for the specific sub-problem of zombie detection at a fraction of the computational cost.

The second anchoring paper, by Li et al. (2025), addresses the problem from the perspective of energy-efficient elastic scaling. The authors observe, in a statement that is central to the motivation of this thesis, that "the default scaling mechanisms in Kubernetes fail to effectively distinguish and manage resource consumption of idle containers, leading to resource waste and degraded system performance." They further note that "Kubernetes' default metrics fail to distinguish between active and idle containers" and that "idle containers continue to consume resources, which significantly impacts cold start latency and overall system performance." To mitigate this waste, they propose an Energy-Aware Elastic Scaling Algorithm (EAES) based on Service Level Agreement constraints. The algorithm integrates a feedforward controller that anticipates workload demand with a feedback controller that adjusts container replica counts in response to observed utilisation, and it explicitly models the energy cost of idle containers by incorporating CPU power draw during container runtime, Power Usage Effectiveness, response-time targets, container processing rates, and access arrival rates into a unified optimisation function. Experimental results demonstrate a reduction of approximately 15.34 percent in energy consumption relative to the default Kubernetes Horizontal Pod Autoscaler. The paper is published in the Journal of Network and Computer Applications (Elsevier), a peer-reviewed venue with an impact factor of approximately 7.5, lending substantial credibility to its claims.

Li et al. (2025) make three contributions that are directly relevant to this thesis. First, their explicit identification of idle containers as a distinct category of resource waste validates the problem definition that underpins this research: zombie containers are, at a minimum, a persistent subset of the idle containers that Li et al. seek to manage. Second, their observation that Kubernetes' native metrics cannot differentiate active from idle containers directly motivates the design of a dedicated detection mechanism, which is precisely what the heuristic approach developed here provides. Third, the energy-consumption model they propose offers a complementary metric by which the economic and environmental benefits of zombie detection could be quantified in future work: once a zombie container is identified and terminated by the heuristic detector, the energy savings can be estimated using the EAES energy model.

However, the Li et al. approach exhibits four critical gaps when viewed through the lens of zombie container detection. First, EAES is a scaling algorithm, not a detection or classification mechanism; it adjusts the number of container replicas in response to workload changes but does not produce a per-container classification of "zombie," "legitimately idle," or "active." This distinction is non-trivial because batch-processing containers, cron-job containers awaiting their next schedule, and cold-standby services may all exhibit near-zero utilisation for extended periods without being zombies, and a scaling algorithm that indiscriminately terminates such containers would cause service disruptions. The five heuristic rules implemented in this thesis — sustained low CPU with memory allocation, memory leak pattern, stuck process pattern, network timeout pattern, and resource ratio imbalance — address this distinction by combining multiple metrics with temporal-pattern analysis to separate genuinely unproductive containers from those that are idle by design. Second, the EAES algorithm relies on instantaneous or short-window metrics fed into a control loop; it does not perform the kind of temporal pattern analysis over extended observation windows (the implementation described in this thesis uses a configurable look-back period, defaulting to sixty minutes, with a fifteen-second sampling interval yielding approximately two hundred and forty data points per metric per container) that is necessary to distinguish a container that has been idle for thirty minutes from one that has been idle for thirty seconds during a legitimate inter-request pause. Third, EAES does not use Prometheus as its metrics source; it operates on Kubernetes' built-in metrics, which the authors themselves acknowledge are inadequate for distinguishing active from idle containers. The heuristic detector developed in this thesis queries Prometheus directly using PromQL expressions for CPU usage rate, memory usage, and network receive and transmit rates, obtaining richer and more granular time-series data than the default Kubernetes metrics pipeline provides. Fourth, the EAES algorithm is embedded within the scaling control loop and cannot be deployed as a standalone diagnostic or auditing tool; the heuristic detector, by contrast, operates as an independent component that can run in continuous monitoring mode or as a one-time audit, producing structured output in text or JSON format with per-container scores and per-rule explanations that enable engineers to review, validate, and tune the detection logic without modifying cluster scaling behaviour.

Taken together, these two papers define a research landscape in which machine-learning-based anomaly detection (Anemogiannis et al.) and energy-aware scaling (Li et al.) represent the state of the art for related but distinct problems: general cluster-health monitoring and energy-efficient container management, respectively. Neither paper, however, provides a solution to the specific problem of zombie container detection, which requires three capabilities that are absent from both: first, a classification mechanism that can label individual containers as zombie, potentially zombie, or normal on the basis of multi-metric behavioural patterns sustained over meaningful time windows; second, transparent, human-readable detection rules that can be explained to and tuned by operations teams without machine-learning expertise; and third, a lightweight runtime footprint that does not require graph databases, model training, or integration with the cluster's scaling control loop.

The heuristic-based approach developed in this thesis fills these gaps through a purpose-built detection system comprising five orthogonal rules, each weighted according to its discriminative relevance — sustained low CPU at thirty-five percent, memory leak at twenty-five percent, stuck process at fifteen percent, network timeout at fifteen percent, and resource ratio imbalance at ten percent — and each producing a normalised score between zero and one that is combined into a composite score on a zero-to-one-hundred scale. The classification thresholds — seventy or above for confirmed zombie, forty to seventy for potential zombie, below forty for normal — are configurable and can be adjusted to shift the precision–recall trade-off according to operational risk appetite. The system ingests metrics exclusively from Prometheus, requires no training data, no graph database, and no model-retraining cycle, and its computational requirements (one hundred millicores of CPU and two hundred and fifty-six megabytes of memory at request level, with five hundred millicores and five hundred and twelve megabytes as the upper limit) are an order of magnitude below the infrastructure demands of the Anemogiannis et al. framework.

The choice of these two papers as anchoring references is deliberate and complementary. Anemogiannis et al. (2025) provide the machine-learning benchmark against which the heuristic approach can be evaluated: by comparing detection accuracy, false positive rate, and computational overhead between the heuristic rules and the Isolation Forest results reported in that paper, this thesis can demonstrate whether a simpler, more transparent approach can match or exceed the performance of opaque machine-learning models for the narrower but practically important sub-problem of zombie detection. Li et al. (2025) provide the problem-domain validation: their explicit statement that Kubernetes cannot distinguish active from idle containers, and their quantification of the energy cost of idle containers, establish the real-world significance of the detection capability that this thesis contributes. Together, the two papers frame a research gap that is both theoretically grounded and practically consequential, and the heuristic detector developed in this thesis is positioned precisely within that gap.

A potential threat to the validity of anchoring the research on these two papers is that neither is a peer-reviewed journal publication in the strictest sense: Anemogiannis et al. is an arXiv preprint, and while Li et al. is published in a peer-reviewed Elsevier journal, access to the full text requires institutional subscription. This concern is mitigated in two ways. For the Anemogiannis et al. paper, the institutional affiliation (National and Kapodistrian University of Athens), the funding context (EU Horizon Europe EO4EU project), the substantial length (twenty-seven pages with twenty-one figures), and the rigorous experimental methodology (N = 100 iterations with quantitative performance metrics) lend credibility that is comparable to conference-level peer review; moreover, the broader dissertation already cites non-peer-reviewed sources including Medium blog posts and GitHub repositories, and an arXiv preprint from a major European university represents a meaningful improvement in source quality over those references. For the Li et al. paper, the Journal of Network and Computer Applications is an established Elsevier publication with an impact factor of approximately 7.5, and the National College of Ireland library provides access to the full text through its institutional Elsevier subscription.

In summary, the gap that this thesis fills is threefold. First, where Anemogiannis et al. (2025) detect active anomalies using opaque machine-learning models, this thesis detects inactive zombie containers using transparent heuristic rules. Second, where Li et al. (2025) address idle containers through energy-aware scaling without distinguishing zombie from legitimately idle, this thesis provides a per-container classification mechanism with five orthogonal behavioural rules that make exactly this distinction. Third, where both papers require substantial infrastructure overhead — graph databases, model training, or control-loop integration — the heuristic approach developed here operates as a standalone, low-overhead Kubernetes-native tool that can be deployed in any cluster with a Prometheus instance. The evaluation methodology described in Section 3 of the dissertation validates these claims through controlled experiments on a Minikube cluster with synthetic workloads representing normal, legitimately idle, and zombie containers, measuring accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, false positive rate, detection latency, and computational overhead against both a simple static-threshold baseline and the Isolation Forest results reported by Anemogiannis et al. (2025).
